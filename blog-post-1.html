What is Conformal Prediction? A Gentle Introduction

Conformal Prediction Tutorial Uncertainty Quantification

Machine learning models make predictions, but how confident should we be in them? A model that outputs a single label gives you no indication of whether it is highly certain or barely above the decision boundary. Conformal prediction is a framework that addresses this gap — it wraps any trained classifier or regressor and produces a prediction set guaranteed to contain the true label with a user-specified probability, regardless of the data distribution.

Why Formal Guarantees Matter

Most uncertainty methods in deep learning — dropout at inference time, ensembles, temperature scaling — produce calibration-like scores that work well on average but offer no formal guarantees on any individual prediction. In safety-critical applications such as medical diagnosis, autonomous driving, or legal decision support, "works well on average" is simply not enough.

Conformal prediction provides a marginal coverage guarantee: if you want your prediction set to contain the true class at least 90% of the time, conformal prediction delivers exactly that — provably, with no assumptions about the model architecture, the data distribution, or the problem type.

How Conformal Prediction Works

The core idea is elegantly simple. Given a pre-trained model and a held-out calibration set, you compute a nonconformity score for each calibration example — intuitively, how "surprising" each example is to the model. You then find the threshold τ such that 1 − α fraction of calibration scores fall below it. At test time, you predict the set of all classes whose nonconformity score is below τ. By construction, the true class will be included with probability at least 1 − α.

The beauty of this approach is its modularity: any score function works. In practice, softmax probabilities, distance to decision boundary, or learned embeddings can all serve as the basis for conformity scores.

Extending to Graphs and Robustness

Much of my own research has extended conformal prediction to more challenging settings. In graph neural networks, the exchangeability assumption underlying standard conformal prediction breaks down due to the dependencies between nodes. In my ICML 2023 and ICLR 2024 papers, we developed methods that restore valid coverage guarantees in the graph setting through careful construction of calibration strategies that account for node dependencies.

A separate line of work addresses adversarial robustness: what happens when a malicious actor perturbs test inputs? Standard conformal prediction can fail badly under attack. Our work at ICML 2024, ICLR 2025, and NeurIPS 2025 develops efficient algorithms that maintain coverage even when inputs are adversarially corrupted.

Looking Forward

Conformal prediction is having a moment. The community is growing rapidly, and new extensions appear at every major ML conference. I am particularly excited about connections to epistemic uncertainty — distinguishing uncertainty that comes from limited data versus irreducible randomness — and to test-time adaptation, where the model itself changes during deployment. These threads are coming together in exciting ways, and I am hopeful that the next few years will bring conformal prediction closer to real-world deployment in high-stakes systems.
