Best Poster Award at ICLR 2026 Workshop

ICLR 2026 Award Test-Time Adaptation

I am delighted to share that our paper CATS: Conformalized Adaptive Test-time Scaling received the Best Poster Award at the Catch, Adapt, and Operate: Monitoring ML Models Under Drift workshop at ICLR 2026. This recognition means a great deal to me and to my collaborators — it reflects months of work at the intersection of two problems I care deeply about: uncertainty quantification and test-time adaptation.

What is CATS?

CATS stands for Conformalized Adaptive Test-time Scaling. The core problem it addresses is this: when a model encounters distribution shift at test time — inputs that look different from its training data — its confidence scores become unreliable. Standard conformal prediction assumes exchangeability between calibration and test data, an assumption that distribution shift directly violates.

CATS recalibrates the conformal threshold adaptively during deployment, using only the unlabeled test stream. By coupling a lightweight test-time scaling mechanism with a conformal wrapper, the method restores coverage validity under a broad class of covariate shifts without requiring any labels from the target domain. The result is a system that knows what it does not know — even when the world has changed since training.

Why Test-Time Adaptation and Conformal Prediction?

These two areas have largely evolved in parallel. Test-time adaptation researchers focus on keeping model predictions accurate under shift; conformal prediction researchers focus on keeping uncertainty estimates calibrated. Yet in practice, both problems arise together: a model deployed in the wild faces distribution shift and needs reliable uncertainty estimates. CATS is our attempt to tackle both simultaneously.

What makes the combination technically interesting is that test-time adaptation typically modifies model parameters or activations on the fly — transformations that break the exchangeability argument at the heart of conformal validity. A key contribution of CATS is a formal characterization of when adaptive updates are compatible with conformal coverage, and a practical algorithm that stays within those bounds.

Workshop Context

The Catch, Adapt, and Operate workshop brought together researchers working on deployment reliability from multiple angles: data drift detection, online learning, conformal prediction, and model monitoring. The range of perspectives made for unusually rich discussions. Seeing practitioners describe real deployment failures — models that looked fine in offline evaluation but degraded silently in production — reinforced the urgency of this line of work.

Winning the Best Poster Award in this setting is both humbling and motivating. It suggests that the community sees value in bridging the gap between the theoretical guarantees of conformal prediction and the practical demands of deployed, adaptive systems. That is exactly the gap I want to keep working on.

Looking Ahead

CATS is a first step. The current version handles covariate shift; extending it to more severe forms of shift — label shift, concept drift, and adversarial drift — is high on my agenda. I am also interested in the multi-model setting, where an ensemble of specialists is deployed and the conformal wrapper must account for model selection at test time. There is a lot of open space here, and I am grateful for the encouragement to keep exploring it.
